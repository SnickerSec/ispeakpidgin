'Try Wait,' 'In a Minute,' and More

What time phrases REALLY mean in Hawaii (Spoiler: Not what you think!)

Time works differently in Hawaii. When someone says "in a minute," they might mean 15 minutes. When they say "bumbye," it could be tomorrow or next week. Understanding Hawaiian time isn't just about learning phrases - it's about embracing an entirely different relationship with time itself.

This guide decodes 15 essential time expressions in Hawaiian Pidgin, explaining what they literally mean versus what they actually mean. Master these phrases and you'll understand why locals are never in a rush, and why "island time" is a real cultural philosophy, not just an excuse for being late. üïêüå∫

1Ô∏è‚É£ Try wait

Pronunciation: TRY wait

Literal Meaning: "Try to wait" or "just wait a moment"

What It Actually Means: Anywhere from 30 seconds to 10 minutes

Usage: Polite way to ask someone to be patient

üí¨ Example Conversation:

(10 minutes later...)

2Ô∏è‚É£ In a minute / One minute

Pronunciation: in ah MIN-it / one MIN-it

Literal Meaning: "In 60 seconds"

What It Actually Means: 5-15 minutes minimum, possibly much longer

Usage: The classic island time phrase - rarely means one actual minute

üí¨ Example Conversation:

Kid: "In a minute!"
(Translation: I'm finishing this level of my video game, so 15-20 minutes)

3Ô∏è‚É£ Hold on / Hol' on

Pronunciation: HOLD on / HOL on

Literal Meaning: "Wait briefly"

What It Actually Means: Could be 10 seconds or 5 minutes depending on context

Usage: Quick pause needed, more urgent than "try wait"

(Pulling over to talk safely)

4Ô∏è‚É£ Small kine wait / Little bit wait

Pronunciation: small KYNE wait / LIT-tle bit wait

What It Actually Means: 5-20 minutes, rarely actually "small"

Usage: Softening the blow of making someone wait

üí¨ Example Conversation:

(Actual wait time: 25 minutes)

üîÆ Future Time - "Later" Could Mean Anything

5Ô∏è‚É£ Bumbye / Bum-bye

Pronunciation: BUM-bye

Literal Meaning: "By and by" or "later"

What It Actually Means: Could be 5 minutes, could be next week - intentionally vague

Usage: Often used in warnings or promises, deliberately non-committal

üí¨ Example Conversation:

(Bumbye = eventual consequence if behavior continues)

6Ô∏è‚É£ Later / I see you later

Pronunciation: LAY-ter / I see you LAY-ter

Literal Meaning: "At some point in the future"

What It Actually Means: Today, tomorrow, next week, or never - context-dependent

Usage: Can be specific plan or vague goodbye

üí¨ Example Conversation:

(Could mean later today, or just a friendly goodbye)

7Ô∏è‚É£ Afterwhile / Aftah while

Pronunciation: AF-tah while

Literal Meaning: "After some time passes"

What It Actually Means: Later today or this evening

Usage: Similar to "later" but implies same-day timeframe

üí¨ Example Conversation:

8Ô∏è‚É£ Sometime / Some time

Pronunciation: SUM-time

Literal Meaning: "At an unspecified point"

What It Actually Means: Maybe never, but let's pretend it'll happen

Usage: The polite Hawaiian "no" without actually saying no

üí¨ Example Conversation:

Person A: "Eh, we should go hiking!"
Person B: "Yeah yeah, sometime we go."
(Translation: Sounds nice but probably not happening)

9Ô∏è‚É£ Wen / I wen do that

Pronunciation: WEN / I wen do that

What It Actually Means: Emphasizes completion - "it's done!"

Usage: Often combined with "pau" (finished) for emphasis

üí¨ Example Conversation:

üí¨ Example Conversation:

üå∫ Understanding "Island Time" Culture

Island time isn't about being lazy or disrespectful. It's a fundamentally different philosophy about the role of time in life. Here's what you need to understand:

üåä Relationships > Schedules

If someone is talking story with you, they're not going to rudely cut you off because "it's time to go." The conversation is more important than arbitrary time constraints.

üèÑ Flow > Force

Things happen when they're ready to happen. You can't force the waves to come or the fish to bite. Same philosophy applies to life.

‚òÆÔ∏è Peace > Stress

Why stress about being exactly on time if everyone's going to be relaxed anyway? Life's too short to be constantly rushing.

Pro tip: If something is TRULY time-sensitive (flights, medical appointments, court dates), locals will say "mainland time" or "haole time" to signal that standard punctuality rules apply.

‚úÖ DO: Add Buffer Time

If meeting at 3pm, expect 3:15-3:30. Build in flexibility to your schedule.

‚úÖ DO: Embrace the Relaxation

Use "waiting time" to enjoy the scenery, talk story, or just breathe. You're in Hawaii!

‚úÖ DO: Specify "Mainland Time" When Needed

If punctuality matters, explicitly say "mainland time" or "haole time" so everyone knows.

‚ùå DON'T: Get Frustrated

‚ùå DON'T: Be the Impatient Haole

‚ùå DON'T: Rush Local Businesses
