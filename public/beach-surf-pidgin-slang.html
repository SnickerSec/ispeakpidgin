Pidgin Slang for the Beach and Surf

From 'Lolo' to 'Ho, Pupu' - Talk Story Like a Local Surfer

18 essential phrases to sound like you grew up on the North Shore

Hawaiian beach and surf culture has its own unique language - a blend of Pidgin, Hawaiian, and surf-specific slang that's been perfected over generations of wave riding and ocean respect. Whether you're catching your first wave or just hanging on the beach, knowing these phrases will help you connect with locals and understand the true spirit of Hawaii's surf culture.

From the iconic shaka to expressions of stoke and wipeouts, this guide covers everything you need to sound authentic at any Hawaiian beach. Learn the words, respect the culture, and ride the vibes!

Essential Beach Greetings

1️⃣ Shaka

Pronunciation: SHAH-kah

Meaning: "Right on!" or "Hang loose" - iconic Hawaiian hand gesture and greeting

Usage: Raise hand with thumb and pinky extended, wave. Universal positive vibes.

Example Conversation:

Surfer A: "Ho, sick wave brah!" (throws shaka)
Surfer B: (returns shaka) "Shaka back, brah!"

2️⃣ Howzit

Pronunciation: HOW-zit

Meaning: "How's it going?" - casual beach greeting

Usage: Use when running into surfers at the beach or in the lineup

Example Conversation:

Person A: "Howzit! Waves stay good today?"
Person B: "Yeah brah, little choppy but still fun."

3️⃣ Shoots

Pronunciation: SHOOTS

Meaning: "Okay," "Sounds good," or "Right on"

Usage: All-purpose agreement, used constantly at the beach

Example Conversation:

Person A: "Wanna go out for one more session?"
Person B: "Shoots! I stay already waxed up."

Wave & Surf Conditions

4️⃣ Da surf stay pumping

Pronunciation: dah SURF stay PUM-ping

Meaning: "The waves are really good/big right now"

Usage: Describing excellent surf conditions

Example Conversation:

Person A: "Eh, check North Shore today!"
Person B: "Ho, da surf stay pumping! Triple overhead!"

5️⃣ Going get barreled

Pronunciation: GO-ing get BEAR-uld

Meaning: "Going to ride inside the tube/wave"

Usage: The ultimate surf experience - riding inside a hollow wave

Example Conversation:

Person A: "Brah, you see dat wave?!"
Person B: "Yeah! Kalani wen get barreled for like 5 seconds!"

6️⃣ Stay flat

Pronunciation: stay FLAT

Meaning: "The ocean is calm, no waves"

Usage: Describing disappointing surf conditions

Example Conversation:

Person A: "How's da surf?"
Person B: "Bummahs brah, stay flat. Maybe go diving instead."

7️⃣ Choppy/Victory at sea

Pronunciation: CHOP-py / VIK-tuh-ree at SEE

Meaning: "Messy, wind-blown waves" (victory at sea = extremely choppy)

Usage: Describing poor wave quality due to wind

Example Conversation:

Person A: "Still like go out?"
Person B: "Nah brah, stay victory at sea. Wait for tomorrow."

Surf Actions & Expressions

8️⃣ Lolo

Pronunciation: LO-lo

Meaning: "Crazy," "stupid," or "reckless" (not always negative)

Usage: Describing someone doing something wild or impressive

Example Conversation:

Person A: "Dat guy wen paddle out in 20-foot surf!"
Person B: "He lolo o what?! Dat's suicide!"

9️⃣ Wen get smoked/Took one lickin'

Pronunciation: wen get SMOKED / took one LICK-in

Meaning: "Got wiped out badly by a wave"

Usage: Describing a bad wipeout or beating from the ocean

Example Conversation:

Person A: "You okay brah? You wen get smoked on dat set!"
Person B: "Yeah, took one lickin' but I good."

Dropping in

Pronunciation: DROP-ping in

Meaning: "Stealing someone's wave" - major surf etiquette violation

Usage: Warning or accusing someone of taking a wave they don't have priority on

Example Conversation:

Person A: "Eh haole boy! You was dropping in on everybody out there!"
Person B: "Sorry brah, I never know da rules."

1️⃣1️⃣ Stoked

Pronunciation: STOKED

Meaning: "Excited," "pumped," "thrilled"

Usage: Expressing pure joy about surfing or the ocean

Example Conversation:

Person A: "Brah, I so stoked! Been two weeks no surf!"
Person B: "Yeah buddy! Let's go charge 'em!"

Beach Culture & Respect

1️⃣2️⃣ Ho, pupu (Ho-pupu)

Pronunciation: HO, POO-poo

Meaning: "Wow, snacks!" or general exclamation of excitement for food

Usage: When someone brings food to the beach - pupu means appetizers/snacks

Example Conversation:

Person A: "Eh, I brought poke and musubi!"
Person B: "Ho, pupu! Mahalo brah, I stay starving!"

1️⃣3️⃣ Slippah

Pronunciation: SLIP-pah

Meaning: "Flip-flops" - essential beach footwear

Usage: Everyone wears slippahs, never "sandals" or "flip-flops" locally

Example Conversation:

Person A: "Eh, no forget take off your slippahs before going in da house!"
Person B: "Oh yeah yeah, my bad."

1️⃣4️⃣ Talk story

Pronunciation: TAWK STOR-ee

Meaning: "Chat," "hang out and talk"

Usage: What locals do after surf sessions - relax and share stories

Example Conversation:

Person A: "Eh, come pau hana time, we go talk story at da beach."
Person B: "Shoots! I bring da cooler."

1️⃣5️⃣ Show respect/Give respect

Pronunciation: SHOW ree-SPEKT / GIVE ree-SPEKT

Meaning: "Be respectful" - especially important in the lineup

Usage: Core value in Hawaiian surf culture - respect locals, ocean, and each other

Example Conversation:

Local: "Eh, dis our spot. You like surf here, you gotta show respect."
Visitor: "No worries brah, I understand. Thanks for letting me out."

1️⃣6️⃣ Haole

Pronunciation: HOW-lee

Meaning: "Foreigner" or "white person" - can be neutral or derogatory depending on context

Usage: Originally meant "without breath" (no aloha spirit). Tone matters.

Example Conversation:

Person A: "Dat haole surfah stay ripping!" (respectful)
Person B: "Yeah, he cool. Been coming here for years."

Safety & Ocean Awareness

1️⃣7️⃣ No make (don't be)

Pronunciation: NO make / don't BEE

Meaning: "Don't" - warning phrase before advice

Usage: Giving safety warnings or behavioral guidance

Example Conversation:

Lifeguard: "Eh, no make go out there! Get rip current!"
Swimmer: "Oh, tanks for da heads up!"

1️⃣8️⃣ Bumbye/Bum-bye

Pronunciation: BUM-bye

Meaning: "Later" or "eventually" - often used in warnings

Usage: "If you do that, bumbye something bad will happen"

Example Conversation:

Person A: "I no need sunscreen, I get good base tan."
Person B: "Bumbye you going get all burnt up and regret!"

Understanding Hawaiian Surf Culture

Hawaiian surf culture isn't just about riding waves - it's about respect, community, and ocean stewardship. The language you use reflects your understanding of these values.

Key Cultural Principles:

  • Kuleana (Responsibility): Take care of the ocean, it takes care of you
  • Malama (Care): Leave the beach cleaner than you found it
  • Lokahi (Unity): We're all in this together - share waves, share aloha
  • Respect the Lineup: Wait your turn, acknowledge locals, learn the etiquette
  • Safety First: Don't paddle out if you're not ready - know your limits

When you use these phrases with genuine respect and aloha spirit, you're not just speaking Pidgin - you're honoring generations of Hawaiian surf culture.

Pro Tips for Beach & Surf Slang

DO: Learn Surf Etiquette First

Know the rules before paddling out. Language won't save you from breaking surf etiquette.

DO: Throw Shakas Generously

When someone lets you have a wave or does something nice, throw a shaka to say thanks!

DO: Ask Locals for Advice

"Howzit brah, I'm visiting - any tips for this break?" goes a long way.

DON'T: Drop In on Locals

Stealing waves is the fastest way to lose respect. Wait your turn.

DON'T: Leave Trash

Pack out everything you bring. Locals will call you out for littering.

DON'T: Paddle Out if You Can't Handle It

If waves look too big, sit this one out. Putting others at risk isn't cool.
