What is Hawaiian Pidgin?

Hawaiian Pidgin (technically Hawaiian Creole English or HCE) is a creole language that developed on Hawaii's sugar plantations in the late 1800s and early 1900s. Workers from Hawaii, China, Japan, Portugal, the Philippines, Korea, and other countries needed a common language to communicateâ€”and Hawaiian Pidgin was born.

Is it "Pidgin" or "Creole"?

Linguistically, it's a creole languageâ€”a fully-developed language with its own grammar and native speakers. However, locals call it "Pidgin," and that name has stuck. We use "Hawaiian Pidgin" because that's what people actually search for and use in everyday conversation.

Why Preserve It?

Hawaiian Pidgin is a vital part of Hawaii's identity and multicultural heritage. It represents the unique blend of cultures that makes Hawaii special. By preserving and teaching Pidgin, we honor the contributions of all the immigrant communities that built modern Hawaii.
